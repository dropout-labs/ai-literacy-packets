# A4 — Spot the AI

> **Pairs with spread 13** · ~20 minutes · pencil only

## What this activity teaches

How to look at a piece of text or image and notice tell-tale signs of AI generation. What "AI feel" actually is — and what it isn't. How to push past surface impressions toward concrete evidence.

## What's on the page

Six short text samples laid out in two columns, three per column. Three are real (written by a human), three are AI-generated. They are NOT labeled. Each is 2–4 sentences long, on a topic relevant to a Central Coast youth audience: a description of a school assembly, a paragraph about a local park, a few sentences about why someone joined a club, etc.

Below each sample is a row of three checkboxes:

- ☐ Made by a person
- ☐ Made by AI
- ☐ I'm not sure

And a single line: **One thing that made me decide:** _______________________

## Instructions (printed on the spread)

> Read each short paragraph. Decide whether you think it was written by a person or by an AI. Then write **one specific thing** that made you decide.
>
> Don't worry about being right. Worry about being able to explain your guess.

## Answer key (printed on the back of the spread, upside down)

The answer key is printed inverted on the back of the page so a youth has to flip the page to find it. It includes:

- Which samples were real and which were AI
- A short note on each sample explaining the tells

## Reflection prompts at the bottom

1. Which samples did you guess wrong on? What threw you off?
2. Which samples did you get right? What was the giveaway?
3. Was there a sample that was AI but felt human? Why do you think it felt that way?

## Discussion prompt (in margin)

> The hardest part of spotting AI isn't catching the obvious mistakes. It's noticing when AI feels too smooth — too on-topic, too generic, too without-rough-edges. **What does "rough edges" look like in writing?**

## Expected reasoning

The youth practices critical reading — moving past "this feels weird" or "this feels real" toward concrete observable features (overly generic phrasing, sudden topic pivots, lists when prose would be natural, perfect grammar without personality, "as a [role]" framings, etc.).

The reflection prompts surface the meta-lesson: **the giveaways change as AI gets better, but the skill of looking for evidence stays the same**.

## Facilitator notes

- Some youth will be insulted if AI fools them. That's a teaching moment — being fooled means the AI is good, not that you're bad at this.
- The "rough edges" prompt is the most important one. AI-generated text is often *too* polished; humans miss steps, contradict themselves, drop in unexpected details, and use idioms that don't generalize.

## Sample design (for content draft phase)

Real human writing samples should come from:
- Volunteer-submitted youth writing (with permission), OR
- Public-domain youth writing collections (e.g., 826 Valencia archives, with attribution), OR
- Original samples written by DROPOUT Labs volunteers in a youth voice

AI samples should be generated with a generic prompt like *"Write a 3-sentence paragraph about [topic] in a youth voice"* and minimally edited only for length.

The samples should be revised every 6–12 months as AI generation quality changes.
