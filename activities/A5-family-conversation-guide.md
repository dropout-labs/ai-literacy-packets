# A5 — Family Conversation Guide

> **Pairs with spread 15** · open-ended time · pencil optional

## What this activity teaches

That AI literacy isn't just personal — it's a household concern. That parents, grandparents, and older relatives have valuable instincts about manipulation, scams, and trust that matter as much as technical knowledge. That the youth can teach as well as learn.

## What's on the page

A set of seven discussion prompts, designed to be read aloud at the kitchen table or shared with a parent/guardian/older sibling. Each prompt is short, open-ended, and structured to invite the family member into the conversation rather than test them.

The prompts are bilingual on facing pages, identical content. There is space below each prompt for the youth to jot a note about what was said — but writing is optional. The point is the conversation, not the worksheet.

## The seven prompts

1. **What's the first thing you remember not trusting on the internet?** (A scam email, a fake photo, a too-good-to-be-true ad?) What were the signs?

2. **Have you ever gotten a phone call or text that felt like a scam?** What gave it away?

3. **If a video showed a politician saying something you didn't believe they would say, how would you check?**

4. **Has anyone in our family ever been tricked online?** What happened?

5. **AI can now make voices that sound like real people.** If you got a phone call from someone saying they were me — but it didn't sound quite right — what would you do?

6. **What do you wish you knew about computers and phones that you don't?** (No wrong answers — the youth can teach back.)

7. **What's one thing you want our family to be careful about together?**

## Instructions (printed on the spread)

> This page is for talking, not writing. Find someone in your family — anyone — and read these out loud together. You don't have to do all of them. Pick the ones you want to talk about.
>
> If you write anything, keep it short. The point is the talking.

## Why the prompts go in this order

- Prompts 1–2 invite the family member to share *their* expertise (recognizing scams, manipulation, untrustworthy media). This positions the older relative as the knowledgeable one.
- Prompts 3–5 build on that expertise to introduce AI-specific scenarios (deepfakes, voice cloning) — connecting AI threats to instincts the family already has.
- Prompt 6 inverts the dynamic — the youth may now know more than the relative about specific tools. They can teach back.
- Prompt 7 closes with shared commitment.

The arc moves from *they teach you* → *AI as a new shape of an old threat* → *you teach them* → *we agree together*.

## Facilitator notes (for partner-site staff)

- This activity does not need to be completed in the partner-site session. It is meant to go home.
- Some youth will not have a family member who is available, willing, or safe to talk with. The page should explicitly say so:

> *If there is no one at home you want to do this with, that is okay. Talk with a friend, a teacher, a librarian, or no one — just think about the questions.*

This line goes on the printed page in both languages.

## Bilingual design

Spanish prompts are not literal translations. Several prompts use idiomatic Spanish phrasing that better fits the conversational register: e.g., prompt 2's "What gave it away?" → *"¿Cómo se dieron cuenta?"* (a more natural Spanish phrasing than *"¿Qué lo delató?"*).

Final translator validation will adjust register, idiom, and dialect.
