# A2 — Build a Tiny Dataset

> **Pairs with spread 8** · ~15 minutes · pencil only

## What this activity teaches

Where the data AI learns from actually comes from. That every dataset reflects choices about what to include and what to leave out. That the people building datasets shape the AI that comes out the other end.

## What's on the page

A blank table with 5 rows and 4 columns. The columns are pre-labeled:

| Person | Favorite food | Favorite music | One thing they're proud of |
|---|---|---|---|

Above the table:

> You are going to build a tiny dataset about people you know.

## Instructions (printed on the spread)

> Fill in the table with 5 people from your life. They can be family, friends, neighbors, classmates, anyone. Write what they would say, not what *you* would guess.
>
> If you can, ask them. If you can't, write what you remember them saying.

## Reflection prompts at the bottom

After the table, three short prompts:

1. **Who did you leave out?** Think about people in your life who didn't make it into your 5. Why?
2. **What if a computer learned about "favorite food" from only your dataset?** What kinds of food would it think are popular? What kinds would it never know about?
3. **If your dataset had 1,000 people from your community, would the answers change?** What would stay the same?

## Expected reasoning

The youth produces something concrete (5 real people, real opinions) and then is invited to notice:

- They had to choose who to include — and that choice shaped the data
- The "favorite food" column probably reflects their family's culture more than the world
- A small dataset gives a narrow view; a bigger one gives a different view
- What gets left out is as important as what gets included

This is the youth's first concrete encounter with the idea that **datasets are made by people, and the people who make them shape what AI knows about the world**.

## Bonus extension (optional, in margin)

> Try this: count how many of your 5 people speak Spanish at home. If a computer learned about your community only from your dataset, would it know Spanish was spoken there?

## Facilitator notes

- This activity gets emotional for some youth — they may realize a family member they love isn't on their list. That's the point.
- The third reflection prompt is the most important one. If a youth only finishes one prompt, it should be that one.
