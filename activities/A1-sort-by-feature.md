# A1 — Sort by Feature

> **Pairs with spread 5** · ~10 minutes · pencil only

## What this activity teaches

What a "feature" is. How AI systems sort and group things by features they can detect. Why two things that *look* alike to a human might be grouped differently by a machine — and vice versa.

## Setup

Printed in the workbook. No setup required beyond having the packet open.

## Materials

- A pencil
- (Optional) crayons or markers if the youth wants to color

## What's on the page

A grid of 12 small line-drawings: a banana, a baseball, a basketball, a school bus, an apple, a textbook, a backpack, a notebook, a calculator, a soccer ball, a lemon, a phone case.

Below the grid, three empty boxes labeled:
- **Box 1: Things that are round**
- **Box 2: Things that are yellow**
- **Box 3: Things you use at school**

## Instructions (printed on the spread)

> Look at the 12 things in the grid. Each thing belongs in **at least one** of the three boxes — and some belong in more than one. Draw a line from each thing to every box it fits in.
>
> Then answer: which thing ended up in the most boxes? Which thing ended up in the fewest? Why?

## Discussion prompt at the bottom

> A computer can only sort things by **features it can measure**. If a computer could only see colors and shapes, which of your three boxes could it figure out by itself? Which one needs something it can't see?

## Expected reasoning

- "Round" and "yellow" are visual features a machine could detect from a photo.
- "Used at school" is a *contextual* feature — it depends on knowing what school is, what the youth carries, etc. A machine doesn't know that without being told.
- Some objects (a baseball, a basketball) are round AND used in sports but maybe not at school. Some (a school bus) are yellow AND used at school but not round.

This is the youth's first concrete encounter with the idea that **AI groups things by features it can detect, and the choice of features changes the answer**.

## Facilitator notes (for partner-site staff, optional)

- There are no wrong answers. Encourage the youth to defend their choices.
- If they ask "is this right?" turn it back: "is the apple round? is it yellow? is it used at school?"
- The discussion prompt at the bottom is the part that matters most.
