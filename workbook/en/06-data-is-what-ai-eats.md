# Spread 06 — Data is what AI eats

> Part 2: How does it work? · spread 6 of 16

## Heading

**Data is what AI eats.**

## Opening scenario

If you wanted to teach a person what a dog looks like, you'd point at a few dogs. Maybe ten. Maybe a hundred. They'd get it.

If you wanted to teach an AI what a dog looks like, you'd show it **fifteen million** photos of dogs.

This is not an exaggeration. That's the rough scale.

## Body

The "examples" we talked about on the last spread — they're what people in AI call **data**.

Every AI system has been fed gigantic amounts of data. The big text-generating ones (the kinds that write paragraphs) were fed roughly **the entire internet**. The face-recognition ones were fed billions of tagged photos. The music-recommending ones were fed billions of "this person played this song after this song."

The thing AI is good at — finding patterns — only works if there's enough data for patterns to show up.

## What "data" actually means

Data is anything you can write down, count, or save:

- Text (books, articles, tweets, comments, your homework)
- Images (photos, drawings, screenshots)
- Audio (recordings, songs, voice messages)
- Numbers (scores, temperatures, GPS coordinates)
- Clicks (what you tapped, when, in what order)

When you scroll through your phone, **you are creating data**. When that data is collected, somebody can train an AI on it.

## The big idea

> An AI system without data is like a brain without any memories. It can't do anything. **The data is the thing.**

## Sidebar — three questions to ask of any AI

When you encounter an AI tool, three questions matter more than how impressive it looks:

1. **What data was it trained on?**
2. **Who decided what data to include?**
3. **What got left out?**

We'll come back to these on the next page.

## Margin note

> "Training" is what AI people call the process of feeding data to an AI so it learns the patterns. After training, the AI can make guesses about new stuff it hasn't seen before.
