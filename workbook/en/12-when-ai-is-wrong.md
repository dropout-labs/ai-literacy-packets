# Spread 12 — When AI is wrong: hallucinations & bias

> Part 4: When it's wrong · spread 12 of 16

## Heading

**When AI is wrong.**

## Body

AI gets things wrong in two main ways. Both are worth knowing.

## 1. Hallucinations — confidently making things up

Sometimes AI just **invents stuff**.

It will tell you about a book that doesn't exist. It will give you a quote nobody said. It will cite a study that was never published. It will describe a law that doesn't exist in your state.

It will not warn you. It will sound certain.

This happens because of how AI works (remember: it's predicting what words usually come after what other words). It's not pulling from a database of true facts — it's generating plausible-sounding sentences. Sometimes the plausible-sounding sentences happen to also be true. Sometimes they aren't.

The technical word people use is **hallucination**. It's a useful word because it describes the experience: AI is "seeing" something that isn't there, with full conviction.

### How to catch it

- **Always verify specific facts.** Names, dates, statistics, citations.
- **If AI gives you a source, check that the source actually exists.** Search for it. AI invents URLs, book titles, and author names constantly.
- **The more specific and the more obscure, the more likely AI made it up.**

## 2. Bias — patterns the data already had

The other way AI gets things wrong is more invisible.

Remember that AI was trained on data. Remember that the data was made by people. The data carries all the patterns those people had — including the unfair ones.

If you train an AI on photos that mostly show light-skinned people, it'll do worse on dark-skinned people.

If you train an AI on resumes that historically belonged to men, it'll learn to favor patterns associated with men.

If you train an AI on text from one part of the internet, it'll know less about everywhere else — and the people there.

This isn't because the AI is "racist" or "sexist" the way a person might be. It's because **the data was already uneven, and the AI learned the unevenness**.

### How to catch it

- **Notice who AI seems to know more about.** What gets the long, detailed answer? What gets the short, generic answer?
- **Notice what AI assumes is "normal" or "default."** Whose names show up as the default? Whose lives?
- **Test the same question, different people.** "Write a story about a doctor named Maria." Then "...named John." Compare.

## The big idea

> Hallucinations are AI **making things up**.
> Bias is AI **echoing patterns that were already in the data**.
> Both are real. Both happen daily. Knowing the difference helps you catch them.
